Negroni

Bold, bitter, and utterly sophisticated. The equal-parts Italian icon that has captivated palates for over a century — gin, Campari, and sweet vermouth in perfect harmony.

24%
ABV

Ingredients

🥃 Ingredients

London Dry Gin
Tanqueray or Beefeater recommended
30 ml
Campari
The irreplaceable bitter heart
30 ml
Sweet Vermouth
Carpano Antica or Cocchi di Torino
30 ml

🔧 Equipment

Mixing Glass
Bar Spoon
Jigger
Rocks Glass
Peeler / Knife
Large Ice Cube Tray

📋 Steps

1
Chill Your Glass

Fill a rocks glass with ice or place it in the freezer while you prepare. A chilled glass keeps your Negroni cold longer.

2
Measure & Pour

Using a jigger, measure 30ml each of gin, Campari, and sweet vermouth into a mixing glass filled with ice.

3
Stir — Don't Shake

Stir gently with a bar spoon for 20-30 seconds until well-chilled. Negronis are always stirred, never shaken — this preserves the silky texture and avoids cloudiness.

4
Strain Over Ice

Discard the ice from your chilled rocks glass. Add one large fresh ice cube (or a few smaller cubes). Strain the cocktail over the ice.

5
Garnish & Serve

Using a peeler, cut a wide strip of orange peel. Twist it over the glass to express the oils onto the surface, then drop it in or perch it on the rim.

🍊 Garnish

🍊

Orange Peel — The Essential Finish

The orange oils are not decorative — they bridge the gin's botanicals with the Campari's bitterness. Always twist over the drink, skin-side down, so the oils land on the surface.

"The Negroni is not just a cocktail — it is a century of Italian elegance poured into a glass."
1919

Count Camillo Negroni Walks Into a Bar

At Caffe Casoni in Florence, Count Camillo Negroni — a well-traveled aristocrat who had spent years in America and London — asks his friend, bartender Fosco Scarselli, to strengthen his usual Americano by replacing the soda water with gin. The Negroni is born.

1920s

The Negroni Family Embraces the Legacy

The drink gains popularity rapidly among Florence's cafe society. Count Negroni's family eventually establishes the Negroni Distillery in Treviso, producing a ready-made bottled version — though purists insist on freshly mixed.

1947

Orson Welles Discovers the Negroni

While filming in Italy, Orson Welles famously declares: "The bitters are excellent for your liver, the gin is bad for you. They balance each other." His endorsement introduces the Negroni to the American imagination.

2013

Negroni Week is Born

Imbibe Magazine and Campari launch Negroni Week — a global charity event where bars worldwide serve Negronis and donate proceeds. It becomes the largest cocktail charity initiative in history.

Today

World's #1 Classic Cocktail

In 2024, Drinks International names the Negroni the world's best-selling classic cocktail for the 10th consecutive year. Few drinks combine simplicity, depth, and cultural weight like this one.

🥂

Negroni Sbagliato

"Sbagliato" means "mistaken" — a bartender accidentally used prosecco instead of gin. The happy accident became a beloved lighter, sparkling variant.

Gin → Prosecco
🤍

White Negroni

Created in 2001 by Wayne Collins. Swaps Campari for Suze (gentian liqueur) and sweet vermouth for Lillet Blanc — floral, golden, and elegant.

Campari → Suze / Vermouth → Lillet Blanc
🥃

Boulevardier

The Negroni's whiskey cousin. Bourbon replaces gin, adding caramel warmth. Perfect for colder months. First documented in 1927 by Harry MacElhone.

Gin → Bourbon
🍫

Chocolate Negroni

Add a bar spoon of creme de cacao. The chocolate notes amplify the vermouth's richness and the Campari's orange undertones.

+ Creme de Cacao
💨

Smoked Negroni

Infuse the glass with cherry wood smoke before pouring. The smokiness complements the Campari's bitterness for a dramatically different experience.

+ Smoked Glass
🫧

Negroni Fizz

Top with soda water for a refreshing long drink. Lighter, sessionable, and perfect for summer afternoons.

📝 Tasting Notes

●Opens with juniper & citrus from the gin, immediately met by Campari's signature bitterness
●Mid-palate reveals the sweet vermouth's richness — dried fruit, herbs, and a touch of caramel
●Orange oils from the garnish lift the botanicals and soften the bitter edge
●Finish is long, dry, and pleasantly bitter — a classic "aperitivo" effect that stimulates appetite
●Silky mouthfeel from proper stirring, never watery. Sips evolve as dilution opens the botanicals

🫒

Castelvetrano Olives

Buttery, mild green olives balance the cocktail without competing. A classic Italian aperitivo pairing.

The bitterness of dark chocolate creates a surprising but beautiful echo of the Campari. Try it after dinner.

🥜
